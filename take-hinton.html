Hinton is worth listening to. The number people quote him on is a coin flip.

Dom · 22 July 2026

impact 4/6

Geoffrey Hinton

Nobel laureate, "godfather of AI" · CNN's State of the Union, clipped endlessly since

AI will have "the capabilities to replace many, many jobs" in 2026. It already replaces call centre work. Every seven months or so, it can complete tasks that used to take twice as long.

I spent my last post arguing that Microsoft's AI chief was overselling it. So let me be even-handed: Hinton is a different proposition entirely. He has no course to sell, no product launch riding on the prediction, and a Nobel Prize for the research that made the thing work in the first place. When he says he's more worried than he was two years ago, that's worth more than a hundred threads.

Which is exactly why the seven-month figure deserves better than the treatment it gets. It's become the load-bearing statistic of the entire "your job is next" genre — and almost nobody quoting it has looked at where it comes from.

Where the number actually comes from

It's not a vibe. It traces to a real, careful piece of work: METR's "Measuring AI Ability to Complete Long Tasks". Their idea is genuinely clever — instead of another unreadable benchmark score, measure AI capability in units of human time. How long a task, measured by how long a skilled human takes to do it, can an agent finish on its own?

They timed professionals on a suite of tasks, ran frontier models from 2019 onwards against the same set, and found the length of task a model can handle has doubled roughly every seven months for six years. That's the number. It's a good number, produced by people doing the unglamorous measurement work most AI commentary skips entirely.

Two things that fall out of the clip

The first is that the tasks are overwhelmingly software engineering, cybersecurity and machine learning work. It's a suite built to test whether agents can assist in AI research. If software engineering happens to be improving faster than, say, running a change advisory board — and there's every reason to think it is, since that's where the training data and the effort have gone — then the doubling tells you about that domain, not about the economy.

The second is the bit that should stop you dead. The headline figure is measured at a 50% success rate. METR are completely open about this: they use 50% because it's the threshold you can estimate robustly, and they note plainly that useful work often demands 80%, 99%, or considerably more nines than that. As of early 2026, frontier models sit at roughly that 50% mark on tasks taking a human expert about an hour.

So the sentence "AI can now do an hour of your work" actually reads: AI completes an hour-long software task about half the time it tries.

In operations, 50% isn't a capability

Here's where twelve years of keeping services running changes how that lands. In service delivery, a process that works half the time isn't a tool. It's a P1 waiting for a calendar slot.

Think about what a coin-flip success rate means for anything that touches production. You can't put it in a runbook, because a runbook that works half the time is worse than no runbook — it manufactures false confidence at 2am. You can't hand it to first line, because the failure mode isn't "it says it can't", it's "it does something plausible and wrong". And the verification cost lands on someone senior enough to spot the difference, which is precisely the person whose time you were trying to save.

None of that makes the trend line wrong. If the doubling holds, 50% becomes 80% becomes reliable, and the argument I've just made expires on a schedule. That's the genuinely uncomfortable part, and it's why Hinton is worth taking seriously where the thread guys aren't. But there's a real difference between "this is coming" and "this has arrived", and the people flattening that difference are usually selling something.

What to do with it

Not "learn to prompt". The move is to get on the side of the line where the 50% problem is somebody's job to solve: deciding what an agent is allowed to touch, designing what happens when it's confidently wrong, and owning the outcome either way. Every point that success rate climbs makes verification, escalation design and accountability more valuable, not less — because the failures get rarer, subtler and more expensive to catch.

Hinton's warning is real. Just read the actual number before you let someone charge you £499 to survive it.
